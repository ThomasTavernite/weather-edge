📖 How to Read This Dashboard

1

We pull forecasts from multiple sources

Every few minutes, WeatherEdge checks the National Weather Service (NWS) and Open-Meteo for their high/low temperature forecasts for each city. These are the same sources professional weather traders use. We average them into a single Consensus forecast.

2

We compare forecasts against Kalshi's market prices

Kalshi lists temperature markets with brackets like "72° to 73°" or "74° to 75°." Each bracket has a price — that price represents what traders collectively think the probability is. A bracket priced at 29¢ means traders think there's roughly a 29% chance the actual high falls in that range. If you buy "Yes" at 29¢ and it hits, you get $1.00 back — a profit of 71¢ per contract.

3

The "← Forecast" tag shows where forecasts point

When you click a city and see the bracket table, the row tagged ← Forecast is the bracket the consensus temperature falls into. This is what the weather models think will actually happen.

4

Signals tell you if the market agrees or disagrees

This is the key part:

✓ ALIGNED — The forecast consensus and the market's most-expensive bracket are the same. Traders and weather models agree. There's likely no edge here.

⚡ DIVERGENCE — The forecast consensus points to a different bracket than what the market favors. This is where a potential opportunity exists. Forecasters say one thing, traders are pricing another. Someone is wrong — and weather forecasts, especially within 24 hours, tend to be quite accurate.
5

Important: Divergence ≠ guaranteed profit

Weather models can be wrong. Local conditions like cloud cover, marine layers, or sudden wind shifts can throw off even the best forecasts. Kalshi markets settle based on the official NWS Daily Climate Report — not the forecast. A divergence signal means "this is worth looking into," not "this is free money." Always do your own research and never trade more than you can afford to lose.
